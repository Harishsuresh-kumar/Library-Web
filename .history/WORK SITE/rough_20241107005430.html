Authors

William Shakespeare

William Shakespeare (1564–1616) is often regarded as the greatest writer in the English language. He was an English playwright, poet, and actor, whose works include iconic tragedies like Hamlet, Macbeth, and Romeo and Juliet. Shakespeare's plays have had a profound influence on English literature and are still widely studied, performed, and adapted worldwide. His work covers a vast range of themes, from love and betrayal to power and ambition.

J.K. Rowling

J.K. Rowling, born in 1965, is best known as the author of the Harry Potter series, which has sold over 500 million copies worldwide. She began writing the series while a single mother living in Edinburgh, Scotland, and achieved global success after the publication of Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone in 1997. Her work has been translated into numerous languages and adapted into a highly successful film series. Rowling's exploration of themes such as friendship, bravery, and overcoming adversity resonated with readers of all ages. In addition to her work in fantasy, she has also written crime novels under the pseudonym Robert Galbraith.

George Orwell

George Orwell (1903–1950) was an English novelist, essayist, journalist, and critic, widely recognized for his works 1984 and Animal Farm. His writing is known for its exploration of social injustice, totalitarianism, and the abuse of power. Orwell’s 1984, a dystopian novel, serves as a chilling warning against the dangers of government surveillance and authoritarianism. His works continue to influence political thought and are studied for their profound insights into freedom and oppression. Orwell's commitment to truth and his sharp critique of political corruption have made him one of the most influential writers of the 20th century.

Mark Twain

Mark Twain (1835–1910), born Samuel Langhorne Clemens, is a celebrated American author best known for The Adventures of Tom Sawyer and Adventures of Huckleberry Finn. His work is characterized by humor, satire, and a deep understanding of American society. Twain's stories often explore the complexities of human nature, particularly issues like race, class, and morality. His sharp wit and vivid characters made him one of the most beloved figures in American literature. Twain’s legacy continues to shape American writing and is a cornerstone of the American literary canon
